# Memo

**To:** Project Leadership and Potential Partners
**From:** Jesse Andrews
**Date:** April 10, 2026
**Subject:** DOE Genesis Project Concept: AI for Coupled Power and Communications Resilience

This project is being developed as a proposal for the U.S. Department of Energy Genesis program, specifically under Topic 16-A, Grid Modeling and Analysis. The concept is an AI-enabled resilience planning system for coupled electric-grid and communications infrastructure. Its purpose is to help utilities, public agencies, and resilience planners anticipate where severe weather is likely to trigger cascading power and internet outages, estimate the severity and duration of those outages, and identify which mitigation investments will reduce disruption most effectively.

The core problem the project addresses is that electric and communications systems fail together during major disasters, but planning tools rarely model those dependencies in an operationally useful way. Damage to feeders, substations, access routes, or backup power can quickly degrade broadband, cellular service, and emergency communications. At the same time, communities already facing higher physical and social vulnerability are often the least able to absorb long restoration times. The project is intended to close that gap by building an AI-based decision system that models how hazard conditions propagate through both power and communications infrastructure and then translates those forecasts into actionable planning recommendations.

The proposed technical approach is to build a multimodal spatiotemporal asset graph that represents substations, feeders, transmission assets, cell sites, backhaul proxies, transportation dependencies, and exposed communities. That graph would be combined with hazard and weather data, outage history, terrain, infrastructure characteristics, and community exposure indicators to support three primary outputs: probabilistic outage forecasts, restoration-lag estimates, and intervention scenario analysis. Rather than producing only a descriptive map, the system would generate forecasts of outage likelihood and duration and compare mitigation strategies such as feeder hardening, generator placement, sectionalizing, route redundancy, tower battery backup, storage support, and mobile communications deployment.

The project intends to accomplish four things in Phase I. First, it will establish a usable data and modeling pipeline for coupled power-communications resilience analysis. Second, it will demonstrate an AI workflow that improves on simpler baseline methods for identifying where the worst outages and longest restoration delays are likely to occur. Third, it will provide a scenario-analysis capability that ranks interventions by expected reduction in outage minutes and critical-community exposure. Fourth, it will validate the approach through a real-world pilot geography and retrospective disaster cases so that the work is positioned for a larger Phase II effort or operational follow-on partnerships.

The most likely pilot geography is Western North Carolina and Eastern Tennessee, using Hurricane Helene as the primary retrospective case. That region offers a credible test bed because it experienced severe coupled infrastructure disruption and includes rural, mountainous, and flood-prone communities where restoration challenges are pronounced. A second retrospective case, such as Winter Storm Uri in Texas, would strengthen the project by demonstrating that the methods generalize across hazard types and infrastructure contexts.

To be competitive in Genesis, the project must be framed primarily as an energy reliability and grid resilience effort, with communications treated as a critical interdependency rather than the sole focus. It must also demonstrate clear AI advantage, meaning the proposal needs to show why graph-based, multimodal, or other advanced modeling methods materially improve prediction and planning compared with conventional tabular models or static risk indices. The value proposition to DOE is that the project would produce a validated planning capability that helps infrastructure operators and resilience decision-makers act earlier, target investments more effectively, and reduce the duration and severity of service loss during extreme events.

The intended outcome is a prototype system that is technically credible, operationally relevant, and scalable. In the near term, it would provide a pilot decision-support capability for severe-weather resilience planning. In the longer term, it could evolve into a broader national framework for understanding and mitigating the coupled failure of electric and communications systems under climate-driven hazard conditions.
